Question 📊 Accounting BIWS
Walk me through the 3 financial statements.
👆 Click card or press Space to reveal answer
Answer
The Income Statement gives the company's revenue and expenses, going down to Net Income. The Balance Sheet shows Assets (Cash, Inventory, PP&E), Liabilities (Debt, Accounts Payable), and Shareholders' Equity. Assets must equal Liabilities plus Shareholders' Equity. The Cash Flow Statement begins with Net Income, adjusts for non-cash expenses and working capital changes, then lists cash from investing and financing activities; at the end you see the net change in cash.

The Income Statement (P&L) shows a company's financial performance over a period — revenue earned, costs incurred, and profit generated. It runs on accrual accounting — revenue is recognised when earned, not when cash is received.
Standard P&L Structure
(+) Other Income / (Loss)
Revenue (Net Sales) Gross sales minus returns, discounts, rebates
(-) Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) Direct material + labour + manufacturing overhead
= Gross Profit Revenue − COGS
(-) SG&A Expenses Sales, general & administrative costs
(-) R&D Expenses Research & development spend (pharma/tech)
(-) Depreciation & Amortisation Non-cash charge for asset usage over time
= EBIT (Operating Income) Gross Profit − Opex − D&A
(-) Interest Expense Cost of debt; tax-deductible
Treasury gains, forex, dividend from subs
= PBT (Pre-Tax Income) EBIT − Interest + Other Income
(-) Income Tax (Current + Deferred) Tax at applicable rate; DTL/DTA adjustments
= PAT (Net Income) Profit after all costs and taxes
(-) Minority Interest Profit attributable to non-controlling stake
= Attributable PAT Profit belonging to parent company shareholders
Key Subtotals — What Analysts Use
EBITDA EBIT + D&A. Proxy for operating cash flow; used in EV/EBITDA, Net Debt/EBITDA
EBIT Earnings before interest & tax. Capital-structure neutral operating profit
EBT / PBT Earnings before tax. EBIT after financing costs
PAT Net income. Drives EPS, ROE, P/E multiple
Adjusted PAT PAT stripped of one-time items for recurring earnings view
EPS (Basic) PAT ÷ Weighted avg basic shares outstanding
EPS (Diluted) PAT ÷ Fully diluted shares (incl. ESOPs, warrants)
India-Specific P&L Line Items
Exceptional Items One-off gains/losses (impairment, VRS, restructuring) — shown separately above tax line
Deferred Tax Non-cash tax arising from timing differences (accelerated depreciation, provisions)
MAT Credit Minimum Alternate Tax credit — set off against future normal tax liability
Share of Profit (Associates) Equity method earnings from associates (20–49% ownership)
Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) Mark-to-market on investments, actuarial gains — bypasses P&L per Ind AS
Key Formulas
Gross Profit= Revenue − COGS Gross Margin %= Gross Profit ÷ Revenue × 100 EBITDA= Revenue − COGS − Opex + D&A EBIT= EBITDA − D&A PBT= EBIT − Interest + Other Income PAT= PBT × (1 − Effective Tax Rate) EPS = PAT ÷ Weighted Avg Diluted SharesDuPont: ROE = PAT Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier
Margin Benchmarks — India Listed Cos
Gross Margin FMCG: 48–58% | IT: 30–35% | Pharma: 60–68% | Retail: 14–18%
EBITDA Margin FMCG: 22–28% | IT: 20–26% | Telecom: 48–55% | Auto OEM: 10–14%
EBIT Margin IT services: 18–24% | Pharma: 18–26% | Banking: use NIM/ROA
PAT Margin FMCG: 14–18% | IT: 14–20% | Retail (DMart): 5–6%
Tax Rate (India) New regime: 25.17% | Old regime: 34.94% | MAT: 15%
Common Pitfalls & Analyst Tips
⚠
Treating EBITDA as cash flowEBITDA excludes capex, working capital changes, and interest. A company with ₹1,000 Cr EBITDA but ₹800 Cr capex has very little free cash. Always check FCF = EBITDA − CapEx − ΔNWC − Interest − Tax.
⚠
Ignoring Other Income in PATMany Indian companies report strong PAT growth driven by treasury income, asset sales, or forex gains — not operations. Strip other income to assess core business PAT margin trend.
⚠
Missing Ind AS 116 EBITDA inflationPost Ind AS 116, lease payments move below EBITDA (to D&A + Interest). Retail and airline EBITDA margins look 400–800bps higher than pre-Ind AS 116. Always compare on like-for-like basis.
💡
Build a clean/adjusted P&L alongside reportedStrip exceptional items, one-time gains, and Other Income to arrive at recurring EBITDA and recurring PAT. This is what institutional investors value on, not reported numbers.
💡
Track operating leverage carefullyA business with high fixed costs (telecom, airlines) has significant operating leverage — small revenue increase → large EBITDA increase. Model contribution margin separately from fixed costs for accuracy.
💡
Revenue ≠ Cash collectedUnder accrual accounting, revenue is recognised at point of delivery/service, not cash receipt. Always reconcile revenue growth with A/R and collection efficiency to check for channel stuffing.
The Balance Sheet is a snapshot of what a company owns (Assets) and how it is financed (Liabilities + Equity) at a point in time. The fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity — must always balance.
Assets Side
NON-CURRENT ASSETS
Property, Plant & Equipment (PP&E) Tangible fixed assets net of accumulated depreciation
Capital Work-in-Progress (CWIP) Assets under construction — not yet depreciated
Intangible Assets Patents, trademarks, software, goodwill
Right-of-Use (ROU) Assets Lease assets under Ind AS 116 — capitalised leases
Investments (Long-term) Equity/debt investments in subsidiaries, associates
Deferred Tax Asset (DTA) Future tax benefit from timing differences
CURRENT ASSETS
Inventories Raw materials, WIP, finished goods at lower of cost/NRV
Trade Receivables (A/R) Amounts owed by customers for goods/services delivered
Cash & Cash Equivalents Cash + short-term liquid investments (<3 months)
Short-term Investments Mutual funds, T-bills, FDs maturing within 12 months
Loans & Advances (ST) Prepayments, advance to vendors, security deposits
= Total Assets Must equal Total Liabilities + Equity
Liabilities & Equity Side
SHAREHOLDERS' EQUITY
Share Capital Paid-up capital (face value × shares outstanding)
Securities Premium Amount raised above face value in equity issuances
Retained Earnings Cumulative PAT not distributed as dividends
Other Reserves & Surplus Capital reserves, revaluation reserves, OCI
Minority Interest (NCI) Equity of non-controlling shareholders in subsidiaries
NON-CURRENT LIABILITIES
Long-term Borrowings Bonds, term loans, debentures due >1 year
Lease Liabilities (LT) Long-term portion of Ind AS 116 lease obligations
Deferred Tax Liability (DTL) Future tax obligation from timing differences
Provisions (LT) Gratuity, pension, warranty obligations >1 year
CURRENT LIABILITIES
Trade Payables (A/P) Amounts owed to suppliers for goods/services received
Short-term Borrowings Working capital loans, commercial paper, bank OD
Current Maturities of LT Debt Portion of long-term debt due within 12 months
Deferred Revenue Cash received but services not yet performed
Accrued Expenses Expenses incurred but not yet paid (wages, utilities)
= Total Liabilities + Equity Must equal Total Assets
Key Formulas & Relationships
Assets= Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity Shareholders' Equity= Total Assets − Total Liabilities Net Debt= Total Debt − Cash − Short-term Investments Capital Employed= Equity + Debt (or Total Assets − Current Liabilities) Working Capital= Current Assets − Current Liabilities Book Value/Share= Shareholders' Equity ÷ Shares Outstanding Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Net Debt + Minority InterestTangible Book Value = Equity − Goodwill − Intangibles
Balance Sheet Quality Checks
Goodwill / Total Assets High goodwill = acquisition-driven growth; watch for impairment risk
CWIP / Gross Block High CWIP = capex cycle; revenue benefit comes post-commissioning
A/R / Revenue (days) Rising DSO = collection risk or aggressive revenue recognition
Promoter Pledge % India-specific: pledged shares >30% = significant risk signal
Contingent Liabilities Shown in notes — can be large (tax disputes, legal claims)
Related-party payables Large RPT balances = governance concern; check notes carefully
Common Pitfalls & Analyst Tips
⚠
Book value ≠ Market valueBook value is based on historical cost less accumulated depreciation. Market value reflects future earnings potential. For asset-light businesses (IT, FMCG), book value is almost meaningless — market values intangibles like brand, distribution, and talent that don't appear on the balance sheet.
⚠
Missing contingent liabilities in notesLarge tax disputes, guarantees, and litigation are disclosed only in the notes to accounts — not on the face of the balance sheet. These can be material (especially for Indian companies facing GST, income tax, or customs disputes).
⚠
Ind AS 116: ROU assets inflate balance sheetCapitalising operating leases adds both a ROU asset and a lease liability to the balance sheet. This increases both total assets AND total debt — affecting D/E ratio, ROA, and Net Debt/EBITDA. Always strip lease liability for clean leverage analysis in retail/airlines.
💡
Always read the notes, not just the face of B/SThe notes contain: contingent liabilities, related-party transactions, segment data, key accounting policies, and breakdown of long-term investments. The most important information in a balance sheet is often in the notes.
💡
Track CWIP carefully for capex-cycle companiesLarge CWIP balances are future revenue generators — but they earn nothing until commissioned. Compare CWIP as % of gross block over time: peak CWIP = peak capex cycle. Commissioning CWIP → revenue + depreciation charge both kick in.
💡
Negative equity is not always distressIn an LBO, equity can be structurally negative from dividend recaps. Platform businesses with deferred revenue can show negative equity. Context: is it structural (business model) or distress (accumulated losses)?
The Cash Flow Statement shows actual cash movement in three categories: Operations, Investing, and Financing. Unlike the P&L, it cannot be manipulated through accrual accounting — making it the most reliable statement for assessing financial health. Starts with Net Income and reconciles to actual cash.
Cash Flow from Operations (CFO)
Start with Net Income (PAT)
(+) Depreciation & Amortisation Non-cash charge — added back
(+) Stock-Based Compensation (ESOP) Non-cash expense — added back
(+/−) Deferred Tax Non-cash; adds back DTL, deducts DTA
(+/−) Change in Working Capital ↑ A/R or Inventory = cash outflow (−)
↑ A/P or Deferred Revenue = cash inflow (+)
(+/−) Other Non-cash Items Provisions, impairments, write-offs
= Cash Flow from Operations True operating cash generation
Cash Flow from Investing (CFI)
(−) Purchase of PP&E (Capex) Core capex — maintenance + growth
(−) Purchase of Investments Acquisition of subsidiaries, JVs, equity stakes
(+) Proceeds from Asset Sales Disposal of PP&E, investments, subsidiaries
(+/−) Change in Investments Net purchase/redemption of financial investments
= Cash Flow from Investing Usually negative (growth requires investment)
Cash Flow from Financing (CFF)
(+) Proceeds from Debt Raised New loans, bonds, debentures
(−) Repayment of Debt Principal repayment of borrowings
(+) Equity Raised IPO, rights issue, QIP proceeds
(−) Dividends Paid Cash dividend to shareholders
(−) Buyback of Shares Share repurchase from market
(−) Lease Payments (Ind AS 116) Principal portion of lease liability repayment
= Cash Flow from Financing Reflects capital structure decisions
Key Formulas & Metrics
Free Cash Flow (FCF)= CFO − Capex Unlevered FCF (FCFF)= EBIT × (1−t) + D&A − Capex − ΔNWC Levered FCF (FCFE)= Net Income + D&A − Capex − ΔNWC + Net Borrowings FCF Yield= FCF ÷ Market Cap × 100 FCF / PAT> 0.8x = high earnings quality CFO / EBITDA> 0.7x = healthy cash conversion Net Change in Cash = CFO + CFI + CFFClosing Cash = Opening Cash + Net Change in Cash
Cash Flow Quality Signals
CFO > PAT ✓ Strong — working capital release or non-cash add-backs exceeding D&A
CFO < PAT consistently ✗ Concern — earnings may not be converting to cash; check WC
CFO negative, PAT positive ✗ Red flag — possible channel stuffing or aggressive revenue recognition
High capex / CFO ratio Capital-intensive growth phase; track when capex normalises for FCF inflection
Consistent positive FCF ✓ Premium quality — compound machines (Asian Paints, Pidilite, TCS)
CFI inflows > outflows Company is selling assets to fund operations — often a stress signal
3-Statement Linkages
How the Three Statements Connect
Net Income (P&L)→Top line of Cash Flow Statement (CFO starting point) + increases Retained Earnings on Balance Sheet
D&A (P&L expense)→Added back in CFO (non-cash) + reduces PP&E on Balance Sheet (accumulated depreciation)
Change in A/R (B/S)→Working capital adjustment in CFO: A/R ↑ = cash outflow; A/R ↓ = cash inflow
Capex (CFI outflow)→Increases PP&E on Balance Sheet + future D&A on P&L (creates a depreciation tail)
Debt raised (CFF inflow)→Increases Cash on Balance Sheet + increases Debt liability on Balance Sheet
Dividends paid (CFF outflow)→Reduces Cash on Balance Sheet + reduces Retained Earnings on Balance Sheet
Net Change in Cash (CFS)→Reconciles to closing Cash balance on Balance Sheet (Opening Cash + Net Change = Closing Cash)
Deferred Tax (P&L)→Non-cash; added back in CFO + creates DTL/DTA on Balance Sheet
Common Pitfalls & Analyst Tips
⚠
Mistaking "cash profit" for free cash flowCash profit = PAT + D&A. FCF = CFO − Capex. These are very different. A company with ₹500 Cr cash profit but ₹600 Cr capex has negative FCF and is burning cash despite being "profitable".
⚠
Ignoring maintenance vs growth capexTotal capex includes maintenance (keeping assets running) and growth (adding capacity). Only growth capex is discretionary. Companies that classify maintenance capex as growth to show higher FCF are inflating free cash flow quality.
💡
Use CFO/PAT as your first earnings quality checkA ratio consistently above 1.0x means the company collects more cash than it reports as profit — excellent quality. Below 0.7x for multiple years = investigate working capital, revenue recognition, or provisioning policy.
💡
Normalise capex for the cycleCapex is lumpy — a company mid-expansion shows depressed FCF. Model a "normalised capex" (maintenance + steady-state growth) to get through-the-cycle FCF. This is what drives long-term valuation in a DCF.
Efficiency ratios measure how well a company uses its assets and manages its working capital cycle.
Ratio Formula What It Measures Good Range Red Flag India Context
Asset Turnover Revenue ÷ Avg Total Assets Revenue generated per ₹ of assets. Higher = more efficient use of asset base. 1.0x–2.0x (manufacturing)
2.0x–4.0x (retail/services)		 <0.5x signals asset-heavy business or underutilisation DMart: ~3.5x (very efficient). Capital-heavy cos like NTPC: ~0.3x — normal for utilities.
Inventory Turnover COGS ÷ Avg Inventory How many times inventory is sold and replaced in a period. Higher = faster-moving stock. 6x–12x (FMCG/retail)
3x–6x (manufacturing)		 <3x may indicate obsolete/slow-moving inventory DMart targets ~30 inventory days (12x turnover). Fashion retail: 60–90 days normal.
Inventory Days (DIO) 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover
or (Avg Inventory ÷ COGS) × 365		 Average days to sell through inventory. Lower = faster conversion to cash. 30–45 days (value retail)
60–90 days (fashion/pharma)		 >120 days in FMCG = serious concern Track trend vs peers. Sudden DIO spike = demand slowdown or channel stuffing.
Receivables Turnover Revenue ÷ Avg Accounts Receivable How quickly the company collects cash from customers. Higher = faster collection. 8x–15x (B2C businesses)
4x–8x (B2B businesses)		 <4x in B2C = potential revenue recognition issue B2C cos (FMCG, retail) should have very low receivables. High A/R in B2C = red flag.
Debtor Days (DSO) 365 ÷ Receivables Turnover
or (Avg A/R ÷ Revenue) × 365		 Average days to collect payment after a sale. Lower = better cash collection. 15–30 days (B2C)
45–75 days (B2B/infra)		 Rising DSO faster than revenue growth = collections issue IT services: 65–80 days normal. Infrastructure/EPC: 90–120 days. DISCOM payments: often 120+ days (risk).
Payable Days (DPO) (Avg Accounts Payable ÷ COGS) × 365 Average days to pay suppliers. Higher = company holds cash longer (working capital advantage). 30–60 days (FMCG)
60–90 days (retail/auto)		 >120 days may signal liquidity stress or supplier disputes DMart pays quickly (low DPO) — but buys at deep discount. Large FMCG cos use DPO as implicit financing.
Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) DIO + DSO − DPO Days between paying for inventory and collecting cash from customers. Lower (or negative) = better. 0–30 days (strong businesses)
Negative = working capital advantage 		Rising CCC = deteriorating working capital management DMart: ~10 days. Zomato/Swiggy: negative CCC (collect before paying). EPC/infra companies: 120–180 days.
Fixed Asset Turnover Revenue ÷ Avg Net Fixed Assets (PP&E) Revenue per ₹ of fixed assets. Measures how productively the company uses its physical assets. 2x–5x (manufacturing)
5x–10x (asset-light services)		 <1x suggests overcapacity or underutilisation of capex IT services: 8–12x (asset-light). Steel/cement: 0.8–1.2x (capex-heavy, normal). Track post-expansion recovery.
Working Capital Turnover Revenue ÷ Avg Working Capital Revenue generated per ₹ of net working capital deployed. Higher = more efficient WC use. 5x–15x (efficient businesses) Negative WC turnover not meaningful — use CCC instead Negative WC businesses (DMart, Zomato) have infinite WC turnover — a structural advantage.
Liquidity ratios assess the company's ability to meet short-term obligations with short-term assets. Critical for credit analysis and distress detection.
Ratio Formula What It Measures Good Range Red Flag India Context
Current Ratio Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities Ability to cover short-term liabilities with short-term assets. Most widely used liquidity measure. 1.5x–2.5x (manufacturing)
1.0x–1.5x (retail/FMCG)		 <1.0x = current liabilities exceed current assets — potential liquidity stress D-Mart current ratio ~0.7x — not a problem because of negative WC model. Context matters enormously.
Quick Ratio (Acid Test) (Current Assets − Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities More conservative than current ratio — excludes inventory which may not be quickly liquidated. 0.8x–1.5x (healthy)
>1.0x preferred for manufacturing		 <0.5x in a manufacturing company = serious short-term liquidity concern Better metric than current ratio for companies with large slow-moving inventory (pharma, auto components).
Cash Ratio (Cash + Cash Equivalents) ÷ Current Liabilities Most conservative liquidity metric — only counts actual cash, not receivables or inventory. 0.2x–0.5x (normal)
>0.5x = very strong liquidity		 <0.1x leaves little buffer for unexpected cash needs Tech/platform companies often hold large cash buffers. Check against upcoming debt maturities, not just CL.
Operating Cash Flow Ratio Operating Cash Flow ÷ Current Liabilities Measures ability to cover current liabilities from operating cash flows (not just balance sheet assets). >0.4x considered healthy
>1.0x = very strong		 <0.2x means the business struggles to fund its own short-term obligations from operations More dynamic than current ratio. Useful for companies with large deferred revenue or seasonality in WC.
Net Working Capital (₹) Current Assets − Current Liabilities Absolute rupee buffer available after settling all current liabilities. Positive = cushion; negative = deficit. Positive and growing with revenue for most businesses Rapidly declining NWC without explanation = cash being consumed Negative NWC is fine for Zomato, DMart (structural). Negative NWC for an EPC company = distress signal.
Defensive Interval Ratio (Cash + Marketable Securities + Net Receivables) ÷ Daily Operating Expenses How many days a company can operate using only its most liquid assets, without any new revenue. >60 days considered healthy <30 days = very limited buffer; vulnerable to revenue disruption Particularly useful for companies with lumpy revenue (project-based cos, real estate developers).
Solvency ratios assess the company's long-term financial health and ability to meet long-term debt obligations. Critical for credit rating and leveraged buyout analysis.
Ratio Formula What It Measures Good Range Red Flag India Context
Debt-to-Equity (D/E) Total Debt ÷ Shareholders' Equity Financial leverage. How much debt is used relative to equity. Higher = more leveraged, more risk. <1.0x (conservative)
1.0x–2.0x (moderate leverage)		 >3.0x in non-financial companies = high risk; >5x = distress territory Capital-intensive sectors (power, infra): 2–4x normal. NBFCs: 4–7x by design (leverage is the business model).
Net Debt / EBITDA (Total Debt − Cash) ÷ EBITDA Years of EBITDA needed to repay net debt. Most commonly used leverage metric in M&A and credit analysis. <2.0x (investment grade)
2.0x–3.5x (moderate)
3.5x–5.0x (leveraged)		 >5.0x = highly leveraged; >7.0x = distress (Vodafone Idea: 8x+) Telecom (Airtel): 2.5–3x target. RE developers: 3–5x. Adani Green: 5–6x acceptable given PPA-backed revenue.
Interest Coverage Ratio (ICR) EBIT ÷ Interest Expense
also: EBITDA ÷ Interest Expense		 How many times the company can cover its interest payments from operating profit. Higher = safer. >3.0x (investment grade comfort)
>5.0x (very healthy)		 <1.5x = potential interest payment risk; <1.0x = cannot cover interest RBI/rating agencies watch ICR closely. Sub-2x ICR in India often triggers covenant breaches and NPA classification.
Debt Service Coverage (DSCR) Operating Cash Flow ÷ (Principal Repayment + Interest) Ability to service total debt obligations (principal + interest) from operating cash flows. Used by lenders. >1.25x required by most lenders
>1.5x considered comfortable 		<1.0x = cash flow insufficient to service debt — default risk Infrastructure project finance in India mandates minimum DSCR of 1.2–1.3x in loan covenants.
Debt-to-Assets Total Debt ÷ Total Assets Proportion of assets financed by debt. Shows financial risk from a balance sheet perspective. <0.4x (conservative)
0.4x–0.6x (moderate)		 >0.7x = majority of assets debt-financed; limited equity cushion Asset-heavy sectors (power, steel): 0.5–0.65x normal. Pure services / IT: should be well below 0.2x.
Equity Multiplier Total Assets ÷ Shareholders' Equity Financial leverage from a DuPont perspective. Higher = more assets financed by debt. Component of ROE. 1.5x–2.5x (balanced)
NBFCs/banks: 5x–10x (by design)		 >4x for industrials = significant leverage risk Central to DuPont ROE decomposition: ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier.
Fixed Charge Coverage (EBIT + Fixed Charges) ÷ (Fixed Charges + Interest) Ability to cover ALL fixed obligations (interest + lease payments) from earnings. More complete than ICR. >2.0x healthy
>3.0x very comfortable		 <1.2x = high fixed-cost risk, especially in downturns Key for retail (heavy lease obligations) and airlines. Post Ind AS 116, lease liabilities are now on balance sheet.
Promoter Pledge Ratio Pledged shares ÷ Total promoter holding (%) India-specific solvency signal. Pledging shares for loans — if stock falls, forced selling can cascade. <10% pledging = low risk
0% = cleanest signal		 >30% pledge = significant risk; >50% = avoid SEBI mandates disclosure. High promoter pledge + high D/E = double leverage risk unique to Indian markets.

A DCF values a business by discounting its future free cash flows to the present. It is the only intrinsic valuation method — not anchored to market prices. Master the build process, not just the theory.
Step-by-Step DCF Build
1
Project Revenue (3–5 years explicit period)
Use the sector-specific revenue model (volume × price, NII × NIM, GMV × take rate). Build a bottom-up forecast — never just apply a flat growth % to revenue. Justify each assumption. Typical explicit period: 5 years for stable cos, 7–10 years for high-growth.
2
Build down to EBIT, then NOPAT
Revenue → Gross Profit → EBITDA → EBIT. Then: NOPAT = EBIT × (1 − tax rate). Use the marginal tax rate (India: 25.17% new regime). NOPAT is the after-tax operating profit available to all capital providers.
3
Calculate Unlevered Free Cash Flow (FCFF)
FCFF = NOPAT + D&A − ΔWorking Capital − Capex
D&A is added back (non-cash). ΔWC: increase = cash outflow (negative). Capex: both maintenance and growth. FCFF is capital-structure neutral — goes to EV, not equity value.
4
Calculate WACC (discount rate)
WACC = Ke × (E/V) + Kd × (D/V) × (1−t)
Use target capital structure (not current if temporarily distorted). India: typical WACC range 10–14% for large caps. See WACC tab for full build.
5
Discount each year's FCFF to present value
PV(FCFFₙ) = FCFFₙ ÷ (1 + WACC)ⁿ
Use mid-year convention if cash flows are generated throughout the year (not just at year end): discount factor = (1+WACC)^(n−0.5). Sum all PV(FCFF) = PV of explicit period.
6
Calculate Terminal Value
Two methods — see Terminal Value tab. TV typically = 60–80% of total EV. Discount TV back: PV(TV) = TV ÷ (1+WACC)ⁿ
7
Sum to Enterprise Value → Equity Value → Price
EV = Σ PV(FCFF) + PV(TV)
Equity Value = EV − Net Debt − Minority Interest + Cash
Price per share = Equity Value ÷ Diluted shares
8
Sanity check & sensitivity
Check: TV% of EV (should be 60–80%). Run WACC × terminal growth sensitivity table. Cross-check implied EV/EBITDA vs trading comps. If implied multiple looks absurd — your assumptions are wrong.
FCFF Formula — Detailed
FCFF= EBIT × (1 − Tax Rate) + Depreciation & Amortisation ← add back (non-cash)− Capital Expenditure ← cash out for assets− Increase in Net Working Capital ← cash tied up in WCEquivalently, starting from CFO:FCFF= CFO + Interest Expense × (1−t) − Capex Levered FCF (FCFE → Equity Value):FCFE= FCFF − Interest ×(1−t) + Net Borrowings

Common DCF Mistakes
⚠
Using current capital structure for WACCIf a company is temporarily over-levered (post-acquisition) or under-levered, its current D/E ratio is not representative. Use the target long-run capital structure or industry average D/E in your WACC.
⚠
Terminal growth rate higher than WACCIf g ≥ WACC the Gordon Growth Model gives a negative or infinite TV — a mathematical impossibility. g must always be less than WACC. In India, g = 5–6% is standard; anything above 7% requires strong justification.
⚠
Ignoring capex in terminal yearMany analysts use final year FCFF as the TV base but forget that a growing business needs capex to sustain growth. Reinvestment rate = g ÷ ROIC. Adjust TV base for reinvestment: FCFFₙ₊₁ = NOPAT × (1 − g/ROIC)
💡
Always run a reverse DCF firstBefore building your own projections, run a reverse DCF: what growth rate is the current market price implying at a reasonable WACC? If the market implies 25% revenue CAGR for 10 years for a stable FMCG company — that's a sell signal.
💡
Cross-check implied EV/EBITDA exit multipleYour terminal value implies an exit multiple. Calculate it: TV ÷ Terminal year EBITDA. If it's 45x for a commodity business — your g is too high. This cross-check catches unreasonable terminal assumptions instantly.
💡
Normalise the final year before applying TVThe last explicit period year should represent a "normalised" steady state — not a peak capex year, not a WC disruption year. If year 5 looks abnormal, extend to year 7 or adjust the terminal FCF directly.
WACC is the blended required return for all capital providers (equity + debt). It is the discount rate in a DCF and the hurdle rate for value creation (ROIC > WACC = value created). Getting WACC wrong by 1% moves your DCF price target by 10–20%.
WACC Formula — Full Build
WACC= Ke × (E/V) + Kd × (D/V) × (1−t) Where: Ke= Cost of Equity (via CAPM) Kd= Pre-tax Cost of Debt E/V= Equity ÷ (Equity + Debt) ← use market valuesD/V= Debt ÷ (Equity + Debt) t= Marginal tax rate ← India: 25.17%Debt gets a tax shield: interest is tax-deductiblePreferred stock: add Kp × (P/V) if material
Cost of Equity — CAPM:Ke= Rf + β × ERP Rf= Risk-free rate ← India 10-yr G-Sec: ~7.1%β= Levered Beta of the stock ERP= Equity Risk Premium ← India: 5.5–7%Example: Rf=7.1%, β=1.2, ERP=6.0%Ke= 7.1 + 1.2 × 6.0 =14.3%
How to Build Beta — Step by Step
1
Get raw (levered) beta
Run regression of stock returns vs Nifty 50 over 2–5 years (weekly data). Or use published beta from Bloomberg, NSE, or Damodaran's dataset.
2
Unlever the beta (remove capital structure effect)
βᵤ = βₗ ÷ [1 + (1−t) × (D/E)]
This gives the "asset beta" — pure business risk without leverage distortion.
3
Relever at target capital structure
βₗ = βᵤ × [1 + (1−t) × (D/E)_target]
Use target D/E, not current. This gives a forward-looking beta reflecting the intended capital structure.
4
Use median of peer betas for private cos
For private companies or newly listed stocks with insufficient history, use the median unlevered beta of 5–8 comparable listed peers, then relever at the target D/E.

Gordon Growth Model Better for stable, mature businesses. Theoretically grounded. More sensitive to assumptions.

TV Sanity Checks — Run These Every Time
Check 1 — TV % of EV
TV% of EV= PV(TV) ÷ Total EV Healthy range: 60–80% Concern: 80–90% Red flag: >90% If TV > 90% of EV: extend the explicit period, or your Year 1–5 FCFs are too low.
Check 2 — Implied Exit Multiple
Implied multiple= TV ÷ Final yr EBITDA If GGM TV implies 60x EV/EBITDA for a commodity business → g is too high. Target: implied exit multiple should be close to current peer multiples. Significant premium needs justification.

A sensitivity table shows how your output (share price or EV) changes when you vary two key inputs simultaneously. It is the most important output of any DCF — a single price target is meaningless without showing the range of outcomes under different assumptions.
How to Build a Sensitivity Table
1
Choose the 2 most impactful variables
For DCF: almost always WACC × Terminal Growth Rate. Alternatively: WACC × Exit Multiple, or Revenue CAGR × EBITDA Margin. Pick variables that your reader most wants to stress-test.
2
Set ranges — ±2 steps from base case
WACC: base ±100–200bps in 50bps steps. Terminal g: base ±1–2% in 0.5% steps. Creates a 5×5 matrix (25 scenarios). Always include your base case at the centre.
3
In Excel: use Data Table (What-If Analysis)
Set up row input cell (WACC) and column input cell (g). Link the table output to your implied share price cell. Excel recalculates all 25 combinations automatically.
4
Apply conditional formatting
Green = above current price (upside). Red = below current price (downside). Circle or bold the base case. This lets a reader instantly see the bull/bear/base distribution.

A Football Field chart visualises the valuation range from multiple methodologies side-by-side on a single price axis. It shows investors where each method lands and how the current market price compares to intrinsic/relative value.
How to Build a Football Field
1
Run all valuation methodologies
At minimum: DCF (with sensitivity range), Trading Comps (EV/EBITDA and P/E), and Precedent Transactions. Optionally add: 52-week range, analyst consensus target, LBO floor.
2
For each method, derive a min–max range
DCF: bear WACC × low g to bull WACC × high g. Comps: apply 25th–75th percentile multiple to your estimates. Transactions: apply discount–premium range.
3
Plot as horizontal bar chart
Each bar represents one methodology. X-axis = share price. Bar spans from min to max implied price. Add a vertical line for current market price. Methodology with the widest range = most uncertain.
4
Draw conclusions — where does market price sit?
If current price is below all ranges → strong buy signal. If above all ranges → expensive. If near the middle of DCF range but above comps → market pricing in growth that comps don't capture.

Trading Comps (Comparable Company Analysis) values a company by applying multiples from similar publicly traded peers. It is the most commonly used valuation method in day-to-day equity research because it is market-anchored, fast to update, and easy to communicate.
How to Build a Trading Comps Table
1
Select 5–8 comparable companies
Screen for: same sector, similar business model, similar size (market cap within 0.5–3×), similar geographies and margins. Avoid including outliers unless you explain why. Quality > quantity.
2
Calculate Enterprise Value for each peer
EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash + Minority Interest
Use current market cap (live price × diluted shares). Use most recent balance sheet for debt and cash.
3
Collect consensus estimates (NTM or LTM)
NTM (Next twelve months) multiples are forward-looking and preferred for valuation. LTM (Last twelve months) = historical. Use NTM for growth companies; LTM only for very stable businesses. Source: Bloomberg, NSE/BSE, broker research.
4
Calculate multiples for each peer
EV/EBITDA = EV ÷ NTM EBITDA   P/E = Price ÷ NTM EPS   EV/Revenue = EV ÷ NTM Revenue
5
Calculate median and apply to your target
Use median (not mean — avoids outlier distortion). Apply median multiple to your NTM estimate for the target company. Implied price = (Median EV/EBITDA × NTM EBITDA − Net Debt) ÷ Diluted shares.
6
Apply premium/discount to median if justified
If your company has higher ROCE, faster growth, or stronger brand vs peers → premium. If smaller, lower margins, governance concerns → discount. Always quantify and justify: "we apply a 15% premium to peer median given superior ROCE of 28% vs 18% for peers."
Multiple Selection — Match Numerator & Denominator
Multiple Numerator Denominator Use When
EV / EBITDA Enterprise Value EBITDA Standard for most non-financial companies; capital structure neutral
EV / EBIT Enterprise Value EBIT When D&A is a real economic cost (capex-heavy: power, telecom)
EV / Revenue Enterprise Value Revenue Pre-profit companies, high-growth SaaS/internet, revenue-stage startups
P / E Market Cap Net Income Stable earnings; most intuitive for retail investors; FMCG, IT
P / BV Market Cap Book Equity Banks, NBFCs, insurance — where equity value ≈ book value
P / FCF Market Cap Free Cash Flow Cash generative businesses; cross-check vs P/E for earnings quality
Common Comps Mistakes
⚠
Using LTM multiples for a recovering businessLTM EBITDA may be abnormally depressed (post-COVID, mid-cycle). Apply NTM multiples to forward estimates. Otherwise your "cheap" multiple is just a distressed trailing number.
⚠
Not adjusting for Ind AS 116Post Ind AS 116, EBITDA includes lease add-backs. If some peers adopted it and some haven't (cross-border comps), your EV/EBITDA multiples are not comparable. Always note and adjust.
💡
Always show the growth-adjusted multiple (PEG)A company at 50x P/E growing 30% p.a. is cheaper than one at 35x P/E growing 10%. Show PEG = P/E ÷ growth rate alongside raw multiples to make a proper value argument.

The 3-statement model (Income Statement, Balance Sheet, Cash Flow Statement) is the foundation of all financial modelling. Every LBO, DCF, and M&A model is built on top of it. You must be able to explain how all three link together in any interview.
🔗 How the Three Statements Link
The Complete Linkage Map
Income Statement
Revenue → Net Income
→
Net Income flows into Retained Earnings on Balance Sheet
AND into Cash Flow Statement (top of CFO)
Cash Flow Statement
CFO + CFI + CFF
→
Ending Cash = Cash on Balance Sheet
D&A added back in CFO (non-cash from IS)
Capex flows to PP&E on Balance Sheet
Balance Sheet
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
→
Working Capital changes flow into CFO
Debt draws/repayments flow into CFF
Must balance every period — if not, model has an error
📋 Income Statement — Key Lines
Line Item What to Know
Revenue Top line. Model by segment/product/geography. Use volume × price drivers.
COGS Direct costs. Gross margin = (Revenue − COGS) / Revenue. Watch for mix shifts.
Gross Profit Revenue − COGS. Key profitability signal at product level.
EBITDA Gross Profit − Opex (excl. D&A). Operating cash proxy. Most-used valuation metric.
D&A Non-cash. Reduces EBIT but added back in CFS. Drives deferred tax.
EBIT EBITDA − D&A. Also = Operating Income. Used in EBIT × (1−t) for NOPAT.
Interest Driven by debt schedule. Creates circularity if debt depends on cash balance.
EBT EBIT − Net Interest. Apply effective tax rate to get net income.
Net Income Feeds retained earnings (BS) and top of cash flow statement.
💵 Cash Flow Statement — Structure
1
CFO — Cash from Operations
Start with Net Income. Add back D&A (non-cash). Adjust for Working Capital changes (↑AR = cash outflow, ↑AP = cash inflow). Also add back other non-cash items (stock comp, provisions).
2
CFI — Cash from Investing
Capex (negative — cash out). Asset disposals (positive). Acquisitions (negative). Changes in investments. Capex links to PP&E on balance sheet.
3
CFF — Cash from Financing
Debt raised (positive) / repaid (negative). Equity issuance / buybacks. Dividends paid. Links directly to debt schedule and equity section of BS.
4
Ending Cash = Beginning Cash + CFO + CFI + CFF
This ending cash balance must equal the Cash line on the Balance Sheet. This is the key check that your model balances.
⚖️ Balance Sheet Mechanics
Key Balance Sheet Links
BS Line Driven By
Cash Ending cash from CFS (the plug)
Accounts Receivable Revenue × (DSO / 365)
Inventory COGS × (DIO / 365)
PP&E (net) Prior PP&E + Capex − D&A
Accounts Payable COGS × (DPO / 365)
Debt Prior debt + draws − repayments (debt schedule)
Retained Earnings Prior RE + Net Income − Dividends
Working Capital — The Formula
NWC = Current Assets − Current Liabilities = (AR + Inventory + Prepaid) − (AP + Accrued Liabilities) Change in NWC (for CFS): ↑ NWC = Cash Outflow (negative in CFO) ↓ NWC = Cash Inflow (positive in CFO) DSO = AR / Revenue × 365 DIO = Inventory / COGS × 365 DPO = AP / COGS × 365 CCC = DSO + DIO − DPO
💡
Interview tip: If a company's DSO is rising quarter on quarter, it means customers are paying more slowly — a red flag for revenue quality. Interviewers love this insight.
The debt schedule is the most complex part of a 3-statement model. It tracks every tranche of debt, calculates interest expense (which feeds the Income Statement), and models repayments (which feed the Cash Flow Statement). Getting this right is what separates good models from great ones.
🏗️ How to Build a Debt Schedule
Step-by-Step Build
1
List every debt tranche separately
Revolving Credit Facility (RCF), Term Loan A, Term Loan B, Senior Notes, Subordinated Debt. Each has its own interest rate, maturity, and repayment terms.
2
Opening Balance = Prior Year Closing Balance
For Year 1: use transaction debt amounts. Each subsequent year rolls forward. Opening → + Draws − Mandatory Repayments − Optional Prepayments = Closing.
3
Calculate Interest on Average Balance
Interest = (Opening + Closing) / 2 × Interest Rate. Using average balance is more accurate than opening or closing alone. This creates a circular reference (see Circularity tab).
4
Model mandatory vs optional repayments
Mandatory: amortisation schedule (e.g. 1% per year for TLB). Optional: cash sweep — excess cash after mandatory payments used to repay debt. Use MIN(available cash, outstanding debt).
5
Link outputs to IS and CFS
Total interest expense → Income Statement (below EBIT). Total repayments + draws → CFF section of Cash Flow Statement. Closing balance → Balance Sheet debt lines.

💡 Key Debt Concepts for Interviews
Term Meaning
PIK Interest Payment-in-kind — interest added to principal instead of paid in cash. Common in distressed/PE deals. No cash outflow but debt grows.
Cash Sweep Mandatory use of excess cash to repay debt. Lender protection. Models as MIN(excess cash, outstanding debt).
Covenant Financial ratio limit (e.g. Net Debt/EBITDA < 4.0x). Breach = event of default. Model a covenant tracker alongside debt schedule.
Amortisation Scheduled principal repayment. TLA typically amortises faster than TLB. Bullet = all principal at maturity.
DSCR Debt Service Coverage = EBITDA / (Interest + Principal). Lenders want >1.2x. Below 1.0x = cannot service debt from operations.
Net Debt Total Debt − Cash. Used in bridge from EV to Equity Value. Always use gross debt for interest, net debt for valuation.

Circular references are one of the most feared topics in modelling interviews. They are common, unavoidable in realistic models, and must be handled correctly. If you say "I just avoid circularity" in an interview — that's a red flag.
🔄 What Causes Circularity?
The Classic Circularity Chain
Why interest expense creates a circle:
Interest Expense (IS) → reduces Net Income → reduces Retained Earnings → affects Cash Balance → affects Debt Repayment (cash sweep) → changes Debt Balance → changes Interest Expense ↩
Other common circular loops:
• Revolver draws: Cash shortfall → draw on revolver → revolver interest → reduces cash → bigger shortfall

• Tax on interest: Interest reduces EBT → changes tax → changes net income → changes cash → changes debt → changes interest

• Dividend policy: Dividend = % of net income → net income includes interest → interest depends on cash → cash depends on dividend
How to Handle Circularity — 3 Methods
1
Enable Iterative Calculation Preferred
File → Options → Formulas → Enable Iterative Calculation. Set max iterations to 100, max change to 0.001. Excel will recalculate the circle until it converges. Clean, no workarounds needed.
2
Circular Reference Switch Common in IB
Add a toggle cell (0 or 1). When 0: use prior year interest (breaks the circle). When 1: use current year interest (model runs live). Formula: =IF(switch=0, prior_interest, current_interest). Prevents #REF errors when model is first built.
3
Use Beginning Balance for Interest Simple
Instead of average balance, calculate interest on opening balance only. Slightly less accurate but eliminates the circularity entirely. Acceptable for quick models or early-stage work.
⚠️
Never leave a circular reference unresolved. Without iterative calc enabled, Excel will show zeros or #REF errors, making your entire model wrong. Always check this setting when opening a new file.
Interview Script — "How do you handle circular references?"
"Circular references arise naturally in financial models — the most common example is interest expense, which depends on the debt balance, which depends on cash available for repayment, which depends on net income, which depends on interest expense. I handle this by enabling iterative calculation in Excel's settings, which allows the model to converge on a solution over multiple passes. In more complex models or when sharing with people who may not have iterative calc enabled, I use a circularity switch — a toggle cell that breaks the loop when set to zero, preventing #REF errors, and re-enables the live calculation when set to one. The key is to always be aware of where the circles exist in your model so you can explain and defend them."
These are the most common errors that interviewers look for — and that cause models to go wrong in practice. Know them cold. Being able to spot and name mistakes in someone else's model is a highly valued skill.
❌ The Most Costly Modelling Mistakes
⚠️
Hardcoding numbers inside formulas — Writing =Revenue*0.35 instead of =Revenue*GrossMarginAssumption. When assumptions change, hardcoded values don't update. Every assumption must live in a clearly labelled input cell.
⚠️
Balance sheet that doesn't balance — Assets ≠ Liabilities + Equity. Most common cause: forgetting to link Net Income to Retained Earnings, or missing a working capital item. Always build a balance check cell (=Assets−Liabilities−Equity should = 0).
⚠️
Mixing time periods — Using annual depreciation in a monthly model without dividing by 12. Using period-end balances for interest instead of averages. Fiscal year vs calendar year confusion for Indian companies (FY = April–March).
⚠️
Unresolved circular references — Leaving circles without iterative calculation enabled, causing the model to return 0 or #REF everywhere. Always check: File → Options → Formulas → Iterative Calculation.
⚠️
Confusing EV and Equity Value — Using EV multiples to value equity directly, or forgetting to add cash and subtract debt in the bridge. EV = Equity Value + Net Debt + Minorities + Preferred − Associates.
⚠️
Wrong sign conventions — Capex shown as positive when it should be negative (cash outflow). Debt repayment shown as positive. Inconsistent sign convention across CFS makes the model wrong even if individual lines are right.
⚠️
Double-counting D&A — D&A reduces EBIT on the IS, then gets added back on the CFS. Some modellers accidentally subtract it twice, or forget to add it back. Always trace D&A through all three statements.
⚠️
Terminal value that dominates — If TV is more than 85–90% of total EV, your explicit forecast period is too short or your near-term FCFs are too low. Extend the period or re-examine assumptions. A TV-heavy model is not a model — it's a guess.
⚠️
Using net income instead of FCF for DCF — Net income is an accounting number. DCF requires free cash flow = NOPAT + D&A − Capex − Change in NWC. Using net income ignores capex intensity and working capital needs.
⚠️
Not stress-testing assumptions — Presenting a single output without a sensitivity table. Every model output is a range, not a point. Failing to show sensitivities signals inexperience. Always build a WACC × growth rate table minimum.

One input, used everywhere
Each assumption lives in exactly one cell. All other cells reference that cell. Never type the same number twice in a model.

Inputs on one sheet, outputs on others

Formulas consistent across a row
Every cell in a forecast row should have the same formula — just copy right. If one cell has a different formula, it will be wrong and hard to find.

BS check (=0), CFS check (ending cash = BS cash), IS check (retained earnings roll). Display prominently. Model is not done until all checks are zero.
